The Riemann Hypothesis: A Window into the Distribution of Prime Numbers Part 1

In this introductory talk, we explore the analytic structure of the Riemann zeta function $$\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s}, \qquad \Re(s) > 1,$$ and discuss how its meromorphic continuation captures deep information about the primes. We explain why the nontrivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$ are expected to lie on the critical line $\Re(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, and how this prediction is closely linked to the error term in the Prime Number Theorem.

The talk emphasizes intuition over technical details. We look at the explicit formula connecting primes and zeros, the role of the functional equation, $$\zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin\left(\frac{\pi s}{2}\right)\Gamma(1-s)\zeta(1-s),$$ and the historical reasons why the Riemann Hypothesis is viewed as a central guiding problem in modern number theory.
